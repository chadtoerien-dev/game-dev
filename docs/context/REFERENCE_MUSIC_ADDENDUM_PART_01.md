PRIMARY GAMEPLAY REFERENCES

Use the following games as directional references, not templates to copy.

Hitman: World of Assassination

Reference for:

- Systemic sandbox environments
- Targets with schedules, routines and vulnerabilities
- Social stealth
- Disguises and access permissions
- Restricted and hostile areas
- Environmental elimination opportunities
- Accident-style eliminations
- Poisoning, sabotage, traps and distractions
- NPC suspicion and escalation
- Witnesses and compromised disguises
- Multiple entry and extraction routes
- Mission stories and discoverable opportunities
- Replayability through different approaches
- Player preparation and equipment selection
- Mastery gained through learning the environment

Hitman 2: Silent Assassin

Reference for:

- Darker atmosphere
- Moral tension
- Isolation
- Deliberate infiltration
- Architectural grandeur
- Religious and operatic dramatic weight
- A protagonist living between public identity and concealed violence
- Music that communicates dread, consequence and fatal purpose

Splinter Cell

Reference for:

- Light-and-shadow stealth
- Sound propagation
- Fluid crouched movement
- Surveillance tools
- Guard distraction
- Environmental awareness
- Close-quarters stealth
- Information gathering before engagement

Metal Gear Solid

Reference for:

- Readable detection states
- Tactical equipment
- Guard communication
- Escalating alerts
- Cinematic storytelling
- Distinctive character personalities
- Blending serious themes with memorable systemic gameplay

Modern third-person action RPGs

Reference for:

- Responsive movement
- Character progression
- Exploration
- Environmental storytelling
- Equipment choices
- Relationship development
- Consequence-driven quests

Diablo and isometric action RPGs are references only for:

- Rapid tactical readability
- Strong ability feedback
- Character-build identity
- Dense environmental exploration
- Temporary tactical camera positioning

The finished game must not become a direct clone of any reference. It must combine social deduction, action, stealth and RPG progression into an original experience.

7. Every target is part of the social simulation

Contestants are not conventional assassination targets waiting inside isolated missions.

Each contestant has:

- Daily routines
- Friends and allies
- Enemies and rivals
- Private meetings
- Challenge responsibilities
- Preferred rooms
- Night-time security arrangements
- Personal habits
- Secrets
- Fears
- Vulnerabilities
- Protection status
- Knowledge of the player
- Knowledge of the estate
- Reasons to change their routine

A contestant’s daytime relationships should alter their night-time security.

Examples:

- A popular contestant may be surrounded by allies.
- A frightened contestant may request guards.
- A suspicious contestant may secretly change bedrooms.
- A contestant who trusts the player may invite them inside.
- A contestant who fears the player may avoid isolated areas.
- A challenge winner may receive protection.
- An alliance may arrange to stay together.
- A manipulated contestant may voluntarily enter a vulnerable location.

The ideal night operation begins during the day.

The player may make a target vulnerable by:

- Earning their trust
- Arranging a private meeting
- Separating them from allies
- Persuading them to change rooms
- Sabotaging their protection
- Creating conflict with their guard
- Stealing their room key
- Learning their routine
- Discovering a secret route
- Planting equipment in advance
- Engineering an apparent accident
- Creating a false emergency
- Framing another contestant

This is a central point of differentiation:

The social RPG creates the assassination opportunity, and the assassination creates evidence for the social RPG.

SYSTEMIC NIGHT-OPERATION DESIGN

Night operations should follow the sandbox philosophy of Hitman while remaining integrated with the wider social game.

Every elimination location should support several approaches:

- Direct confrontation
- Silent close-range elimination
- Poison
- Environmental accident
- Sabotage
- Remote trap
- Disguised access
- Socially engineered access
- Hidden passage
- Long-range attack
- Security manipulation
- Guard diversion
- Target isolation
- Framing another contestant
- Abandoning the operation when conditions become too dangerous

Each approach should produce different evidence.

Examples:

Poison may produce:

- Missing ingredients
- A contaminated glass
- Kitchen witnesses
- Suspicion towards someone with food access

Forced entry may produce:

- Damaged locks
- Noise reports
- Tool marks
- Missing security equipment

Disguised entry may produce:

- A witness remembering clothing
- A misplaced uniform
- Security access logs
- Suspicion towards the person normally associated with that disguise

An accident may produce:

- Evidence of sabotage
- Witnesses to earlier preparation
- Unexplained access to the location
- Reduced immediate suspicion if executed cleanly

A clean operation should require planning, knowledge and controlled execution.

The game should reward mastery of:

- Target routines
- Estate architecture
- Security systems
- Social relationships
- Equipment
- Environmental interactions
- Escape routes
- Evidence management

Do not make firearm combat the default or optimal solution.

MUSIC AND SCORE DIRECTION

Music is a core part of the game’s identity.

The score should be cinematic, dramatic, operatic and psychologically tense.

Hitman 2: Silent Assassin is an important emotional reference, particularly its sense of:

- Sacred grandeur
- Moral consequence
- Tragedy
- Isolation
- Controlled menace
- Ritual
- Elegance
- Inevitable violence
- Large-scale cinematic drama

The soundtrack must be wholly original.

Do not reproduce or closely imitate:

- Existing melodies
- Recognisable themes
- Chord progressions strongly associated with a specific soundtrack
- Vocal arrangements
- Orchestration patterns that would make a track confusingly similar
- Sound recordings from any reference game

ORIGINAL MUSICAL IDENTITY

Develop a distinct musical language using a combination of:

- Full orchestra
- Low strings
- Solo cello
- Contrabass
- French horns
- Bass trombones
